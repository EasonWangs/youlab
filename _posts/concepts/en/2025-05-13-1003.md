In academic literature, figures are critical visual elements for presenting research findings and conveying complex information. Well-prepared figures adhering to established norms significantly enhance a paper's professionalism and readability.

#### 1. Fundamental Standards for Figures

##### High Resolution:

<p class="article-caption">Figures must possess high resolution. For raster images (e.g., TIFF, PNG), a minimum of 300 Dots Per Inch (DPI) is typically required. Vector graphics (e.g., PDF, EPS, SVG), which permit lossless scaling, are highly recommended. Low-resolution figures compromise professional appearance and obscure critical details.</p>

##### Legible Font Size:

All text within the figure must remain legible when scaled to its final publication dimensions. As a general guideline, no text element should be smaller than 6-8 points post-scaling. Verification by on-screen resizing is advised.

##### Concise and Accurate Text:

Text integrated within the figure should be minimal, clear, and accurate. Technical jargon should be avoided or clarified in the figure caption. Meticulous proofreading for typographical errors is essential.

##### Order of Captions:

The sequence of figure captions presented in the manuscript must correspond to the order of their citation within the textual narrative.

#### 2. Guidelines for Multi-Panel Figure Arrangement

##### Strict Alignment:

For multi-panel figures (e.g., comprising subplots Fig 1a, 1b, 1c), precise horizontal and vertical alignment of all subplot borders is mandatory. This imparts a professional and organized aesthetic. Utilization of alignment tools within graphics software is recommended.

##### Whitespace and Balance:

Adequate whitespace (margins) must be maintained around the figure periphery and between adjacent subplots. Text and data points should not be positioned in close proximity to the edges. The overall layout must convey balance and clarity, avoiding visual clutter.

##### Borders and Inward-Facing Ticks:

Enclosing the entire figure or key subplots within a border (box) is often advisable to clearly delineate their spatial extent. The use of inward-facing axis ticks (oriented towards the data plotting area) may confer a cleaner appearance compared to outward-facing ticks.

#### 3. Color Schemes and Contrast

##### Professional and Clean Color Scheme:

Select a professional and clean color scheme. Avoid excessively vibrant or distracting colors. Employ a limited palette (e.g., 2-4 primary colors) and ensure chromatic harmony. Many journals provide recommended color palettes.

##### High Contrast for Differentiation:

High contrast must be ensured between different data series, and between data elements and the background, to facilitate easy differentiation by the reader.

##### Accessibility:

Consideration for color-blind individuals is important. Utilize palettes that remain distinguishable for individuals with common forms of color vision deficiency. Figures should also be tested in grayscale to confirm their continued interpretability.

#### 4. Consistent Font Type

##### Consistent Use of Sans-Serif Fonts:

All textual elements within figures (titles, axis labels, legends, annotations) must utilize a consistent sans-serif font (e.g., Arial, Helvetica). Such fonts are generally considered optimal for readability in both digital and print media for graphical representations.

##### Adherence to Journal Recommendations:

The chosen font should ideally align with journal recommendations or maintain consistency with the main manuscript text font if no specific figure font is stipulated.

#### 5. Font Size Standardization

##### Consistency and Hierarchy:

Font sizes within a single figure must be consistent for analogous elements and hierarchical for disparate elements (e.g., axis labels may be marginally larger than tick labels; a figure title, if present within the image, may be larger still). The number of distinct font sizes employed should be minimized.

##### Legibility at Publication Size:

It is imperative to ensure all text remains legible when the figure is scaled to its final publication size (typically, text should not be smaller than 6-8 points). This requires careful verification. Text that is difficult to read at the intended scale must be enlarged.

#### 6. Standards for Axis Labels and Units

##### Clear and Complete Labels:

All axes must bear clear, descriptive labels explicitly stating the quantity being plotted, precluding ambiguity for the reader.

##### Clear Specification of Units:

Units must be unequivocally specified for all plotted quantities. Standard abbreviations (e.g., "kg," "m/s," "µM") should be used, typically enclosed in parentheses following the label or separated by a virgule (e.g., "Temperature (°C)" or "Concentration / M").

#### 7. Standards for Axis Tick Marks & Grid Lines

##### Clear and Appropriately Spaced Tick Marks:

Axis tick marks must be distinct, of an appropriate number (neither too sparse nor too dense), and spaced reasonably to facilitate interpretation of the scale. Major and minor ticks may be employed.

##### Subtle Grid Lines:

If background grid lines are incorporated, they should be fine and light-colored (e.g., light gray) to prevent obscuring data points or lines. The data must always remain the primary visual focus.

#### 8. Legend Standards

##### Clear Explanation:

The legend must unambiguously explain all symbols, lines, colors, or patterns employed within the figure to denote different data sets or experimental conditions. Wording should be concise and unequivocal.

##### Strategic Placement:

The legend should be positioned so as not to obscure any data elements and to be readily locatable by the reader. Common placements include a vacant area within the plot or, if space permits, neatly external to the plot area.

#### 9. Advanced Standards for Multi-Panel Figures

##### Consistent Subplot Labeling:

For multi-panel figures, subplots must be labeled consistently using lowercase or uppercase letters (e.g., a, b, c... or A, B, C...), often enclosed in parentheses (e.g., (a), (b)). These labels should be placed in a uniform position on each subplot (e.g., the top-left corner).

##### Similar Sizing and Style:

Similar sizing and stylistic attributes (e.g., font types, line weights, symbol sizes) must be maintained across all panels within a composite figure to ensure visual congruity.

##### Logical Arrangement:

Subplots should be arranged in a logical sequence that supports the narrative of the presented results (e.g., left-to-right, top-to-bottom progression).

#### 10. Avoiding "Chart Junk"

##### Elimination of Non-Essential Decorative Elements:

All superfluous decorative elements that do not contribute to information conveyance must be eliminated. This includes patterned backgrounds (solid, light colors are preferable if a background is essential), distracting 3D effects (unless 3D representation is integral to the data, such as for molecular structures), excessive or discordant colors, and non-informative lines or graphic embellishments.

#### 11. Core Principle: Maximizing the Data-Ink Ratio

##### Maximization of Data-Ink Ratio:

A primary objective is to achieve a high “Data-Ink Ratio.” This principle, advocated by Edward Tufte, posits that the preponderance of "ink" (or pixels) utilized in figure generation should be devoted to the display of data, rather than to non-data elements such as excessive gridlines or ornamentation. This maximizes the efficiency of information transfer.

##### Self-Correction and Optimization:

If a figure is perceived as "too weak" (deficient in information content) or "too complex" (difficult to comprehend), it typically indicates a need for further optimization. Options include refining data processing methodologies, relocating certain details to Supplementary Information (SI), or devising a more concise and effective graphical representation.

Following these guidelines will assist researchers in producing scientific figures that are both compliant with academic standards and effective in communicating information, thereby enhancing the overall quality and impact of their research.
